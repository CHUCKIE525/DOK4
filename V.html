Amendment
  V

The right to not confess to a crime (you don’t have to talk). You don’t have to “convict yourself”. The right to refuse to answer questions or provide information to law enforcement or other officials to be used against you in the court of law.

   Chavez v. Martinez - Two officers confronted Oliverio Martinez at the scene of suspected drug-trafficking activity. Martinez struggled with the two officers, grabbing one’s pistol in the conflict. The other officer proceeded to shoot Martinez multiple times, causing permanent paralysis and blindness. The two officers arrested Martinez and escorted him to the hospital, where they later came back for questioning. He refused to answer questions and said “I’m not telling you anything until they treat me,” yet they still proceeded to ask him questions. Martinez eventually confessed to taking one of the officer’s guns. At no point during the interrogation, did the officers read Martinez’s Miranda rights. He was released from custody and not charged with any crime. Martinez filed a federal civil rights lawsuit against Chavez (the man questioning him), pursuing money damages. Martinez claimed that Chavez violated his 5th amendment rights. Martinez went through 2 courts before getting to the supreme court, which ultimately ruled that his rights weren’t violated as they were never used against him in the court of law.
