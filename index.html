The Constitution

The constitution is a collection of the rules for our government and the rights of the people. It is composed of a preamble, 7 articles, each having multiple sections, the bill of rights, and multiple amendments that have been added since its original creation. While amendments can be added and removed, the constitution is considered the “Supreme Law” of the United States of America.

by Mindflash.

The Articles

1. Describes the legislative branch. Sets rules for the House of Representatives and the Senate.

2. Describes the executive branch. Sets laws for the president and vice president. Describes the president and their duties, along with the term that they serve. Talks about the powers of the president, the pardoning rules, nominating ambassadors, judges, and officers, and the impeachment laws.

3. Describes the Judicial Branch. Establishes the supreme court. Notes that all judges are appointed to serve for life.

4. Defines the relationship between the states and says that the “federal government guarantees each state a republican form of government”. States that we are not a "direct democracy" but a republic.

5. Explains how Amendments are passed and created. “An amendment requires two-thirds of both House and Senate and three-fourths of the state legislatures for approval.”

6. States that the Constitution is the “supreme law of the land”

7. Declares that the Constitution is confirmed once 9 of the thirteen states agree to it.

The Bill Of Rights

1. Individual Rights: Freedom of Speech, Freedom of Religion, Freedom of the press, Right to peacefully assemble, and the right to petition the Government

2. The right to carry a weapon (bear arms)

3. The right to not have soldiers stay in a person’s house without their permission.

4. The right to not have your property searched or seized without a warrant issued with probable cause.

5. The right to not confess to a crime (you don’t have to talk).

6. The right to know what you are getting arrested for, to get a lawyer provided by the state, to be able to confront your witnesses, and the right to not wait in prison for your trial over a long period of time.

7. The right to have your case examined and ruled by a jury (for capital punishments).

8. The right to not have to pay excessive bail or receive unusual and cruel punishments.

9. The right to other rights that aren’t part of the bill of rights.

10.The right to the states to hold power that was not invested in the federal government.
